\chapter*{Abstract}

This report presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of a privacy-preserving end-to-end verifiable blockchain-based electronic voting system with anonymous credential management. The system addresses fundamental challenges in electronic voting, namely voter privacy, vote integrity, resistance to double-voting, and public verifiability of results -through a novel combination of zero-knowledge proofs, Merkle tree-based membership verification, and a purpose-built blockchain infrastructure.

The core innovation lies in the application of the UltraHonk zero-knowledge proving scheme (implemented via the Noir domain-specific language and Barretenberg proving backend) to enable voters to prove their eligibility and cast votes without revealing their identity. The cryptographic architecture combines a Poseidon hash-based commitment scheme for voter registration, a Lean Incremental Merkle Tree for on-chain commitment storage, and a nullifier-based double-vote prevention mechanism -all verified through a single zero-knowledge proof generated entirely in the voter's browser using WebAssembly.

The system architecture consists of two major parallel development tracks: (1) a ZK voting application layer with smart contracts, browser-based proof generation, and a React frontend; and (2) a custom purpose-built blockchain in Go, designed specifically for election administration without the overhead of general-purpose platforms like Ethereum.

The functional prototype demonstrates that zero-knowledge proofs can be generated entirely client-side and verified on-chain in a single transaction, achieving voter anonymity and public auditability simultaneously. The system prevents double-voting through deterministic nullifier tracking while maintaining complete voter privacy-a property that is mathematically guaranteed rather than dependent on trusted third parties.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}
Electronic voting system has come out as a possible alternative that can help to modernize the way democracy is conducted by providing efficiency, accessibility and cheapness over the traditional paper based voting methods. Nonetheless, the implementation of e-voting systems has been deterred by valid issues of security, privacy, transparency and trust of people.\cite{hajian2024blockchain}. Generally, e-voting has three major components namely the electoral committee that oversees elections, the e-voting system infrastructure and the voters who cast their votes electronically.

The use of technology should be involved in the voting process with an intention of accelerating the voting process, making it more effective and manageable, assuring privacy, trust, and security, improving usability, and performing better voter engagement. Customary e-voting systems normally use single points of centralization which make them prone to manipulation, hacking, and attacks.

\subsection{Formal Requirements for Secure Electronic Voting}

A secure electronic voting system should meet a number of formal conditions set by decades of research in cryptographic voting theory \cite{benaloh1987verifiable, chaum1981untraceable, adida2008helios}. At times, these requirements are in conflict and meeting them simultaneously is one of the basic problems in the field:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Eligibility:}  Only voters who are authorized are allowed to vote. Formally, $\forall v_i \in \text{Voters}: \text{canVote}(v_i) \iff v_i \in \mathcal{R}$ where $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of registered voters.
    
    \item \textbf{Uniqueness (One-Person-One-Vote):} Each eligible voter can cast at most one vote. $\forall v_i \in \mathcal{R}: |\text{votes}(v_i)| \leq 1$.
    
    \item \textbf{Privacy (Ballot Secrecy):} It is not possible for any entity to know who voted for which candidate. In formal terms, it is hard for any polynomial-time adversary to distinguish between the joint distribution of (voter identity, vote value) and uniform.
    
    \item \textbf{Individual Verifiability:} Each voter can verify their vote was cast.
    
    \item \textbf{Universal Verifiability:} It is possible for any observer to see that the published result accurately represents the set of valid votes cast.
    
    \item \textbf{Coercion Resistance:} A voter is unable to convince a coercer of who he voted for or how he voted (no receipt that connects voter to vote choice).
    
    \item \textbf{Robustness:} It is robust in the sense that the system gives correct answers even if some of its participants (voters, servers, or observers) do not follow the protocol.
    
    \item \textbf{Fairness:} No count of the results comes until after the election has ended.
\end{enumerate}

The traditional contrast of verifiability and privacy is the central paradox of e-voting: how could the votes be verifiable, while being secret at the same time? This project shows that such a paradox can be overcome with the help of zero-knowledge proofs: proofs of correctness that do not reveal the underlying data.

\subsection{Blockchain as an Election Infrastructure}

It is noteworthy that Blockchain technology was originally envisioned by Nakamoto (2008) \cite{nakamoto2008bitcoin} as the basis for the Bitcoin cryptocurrency. Bitcoin is a distributed append-only ledger that is maintained by consensus among distrusted peers. Buterin (2014) \cite{buterin2014ethereum}continued this idea with Ethereum and added to it a capability to implement deterministic state transitions that do not require trusted intermediaries: smart contracts \cite{szabo1997smart}. Wood (2014) \cite{wood2014ethereum} has developed the Ethereum Virtual Machine specification, allowing for verifiable computation on a decentralized network.

Blockchain offers a verifiable, transparent, decentralized ledger that can overcome many of the weaknesses inherent in centralized e-voting solutions. When combined with advanced cryptographic techniques—particularly zero-knowledge. In the field of voting, blockchain provides a way to create a voting system that guarantees security, verifiability, and voter anonymity \cite{mdpi2024evoting, cluster2024blockchain}.

The core blockchain properties relevant to e-voting include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Immutability:} A vote is final after being cast and cannot be changed or deleted. The blocks reference the cryptographic hash of the previous block, forming a tamper evident chain.
    \item \textbf{Transparency:} All stakeholders are able to check the integrity of the process. The ledger is public and can be independently audited.
    \item \textbf{Decentralization:} No single entity controls the election outcome. Consensus mechanisms distribute trust across multiple nodes.
    \item \textbf{Traceability:} The ability to follow the whole election process, keeping voter anonymity with cryptographic methods.
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Execution:} Smart contracts ensure that the same input always yields the same output, get rid of human judgment in counting votes.
\end{itemize}

But the usage of general purpose blockchains (such as Ethereum) results in unnecessary costs: voters need to hold cryptocurrencies to pay the gas fees, management of wallets makes it more difficult, and the Turing complete execution environment is over-designed for the relatively simple logic of vote counting. This observation 2 suggests a two-pronged strategy, a ZK voting application for correctness verification and a dedicated blockchain for efficient election administration.
Smart contracts (or equivalent deterministic state machines) can be used to automate key aspects of the election process, including voter registration, vote casting, and vote counting, thereby reducing the need for manual effort and the potential for election tampering.

\subsection{Zero-Knowledge Proofs: The Key Enabler}

Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) are a type of cryptographic protocol in which a prover can convince a verifier that a statement is true without revealing any information beyond the truth of the statement. The idea is paradoxical, but was first proposed by Goldwasser, Micali and Rackoff in 1985 and has since proven to be one of the most useful ideas in contemporary cryptography. 
There are three essential properties of a zero-knowledge proof:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Completeness:} If the statement is true,then an honest prover can convince the verifier
    \item \textbf{Soundness:} If the statement is false, no cheating prover can convince the verifier (except with negligible probability)
    \item \textbf{Zero-Knowledge:} The verifier learns nothing beyond the truth of the statement
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{ZKPs in E-Voting Context}

In electronic voting, ZKPs enable a voter to prove:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item They are a registered, eligible voter (membership proof)
    \item They have not voted before (nullifier uniqueness)
    \item They have a volid vote (vote binding)
\end{enumerate}

All without revealing which registered voter they are. This is a property that is fundamentally different from the traditional encryption-based methods in which a trusted party will have to decrypt votes at some point. Unlike traditional voting systems, ZKPs ensure voter privacy without relying on a trusted party, and the system operator cannot even determine who voted for what.

\subsubsection{A Concrete ZK Example: Proving Knowledge of a Hash Preimage}

Let's look at the simplest ZK statement that is relevant to our system: A Concrete ZK Example: Proving Knowledge of a Hash Preimage. A voter would like to show that he/she knows a secret value s for which H(s) = h, where h is known to the public and H is a hash function:

\begin{equation}
\text{Statement: } \exists \, s \text{ such that } H(s) = h
\label{eq:hash_preimage}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Without ZK:} The voter reveals $s$; anyone can compute $H(s)$ and check if it equals $h$. But now everyone knows $s$.

\textbf{With ZK:} The voter generates a proof $\pi$ such that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Anyone can verify $\pi$ against $h$ and confirm the voter knows $s$
    \item Nobody can extract $s$ from $\pi$ (computationally infeasible)
    \item The proof is \textit{succinct}: $|\pi| \ll |s|$ (much smaller than the witness)
\end{itemize}

In our voting system, this is extended to proving knowledge of a Merkle path: the voter proves that they have a commitment in the voter registry tree, but without revealing which leaf it is.

\section{Problem Statement}
Conventional e-voting systems face major challenges that undermine the trust, security, and transparency of electoral processes:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Identity Management and Authentication:} Despite this being a major challenge,there is need to make sure that every voter is identified and authenticated. The systems should not allow duplication of registration nor impersonation and how the voter privacy is maintained. \cite{jetir2025secureblockchain}\cite{sharp2024blockchain_evoting}.
    
    \item \textbf{Privacy and Anonymity:} Protecting voter identity and vote secrecy is critical. Transparency and the preservation of anonymity are in a conflict situation and thus it is hard to avoid vote buying, coercion, or tampering \cite{hajian2024blockchain}\cite{kho2022review}.
    
    \item \textbf{Trust and Centralization:} Conventional systems of election are based on centralized authorities that can be vulnerable to insider attack, corruption or manipulation \cite{hajian2024blockchain}. Reliance on third parties of trust leads to single points of failure.
    
    \item \textbf{Vote Selling and Coercion:}  Voters can become pressured or be offered incentives to be in control of their voting credentials which creates threats of vote selling or coercion \cite{hajian2024blockchain}\cite{wang2024multiparty}.
    
    \item \textbf{Scalability and Performance:} Elections can be conducted with millions of concurrent voters, and this can cause network overload, slow vote-submission, and poor system performance \cite{prajapat2020investigating}\cite{mdpi2022scalability}\cite{abuidris2021secure}.
    
    \item \textbf{Individual and Universal Verifiability:} It is a complicated issue to make sure that the total election outcomes are correct and that voters are able to verify that their votes are correctly counted without affecting the privacy \cite{hajian2024blockchain}\cite{wang2024multiparty}.
    
    \item \textbf{Transparency and Auditability:} The problem is to make the process of election auditable and transparent as well as to assure the anonymity of the voter is a challenging balance to consider \cite{hajian2024blockchain}.
    
    \item \textbf{Usability and Accessibility:} Voters have varying technical skills. Complex systems might restrict the access of users who are either not technologically adept or those users with accessibility requirements \cite{hajian2024blockchain}\cite{vladucu2023evoting}.
    
    \item \textbf{Security Vulnerabilities and Attack Prevention:} Election systems have security vulnerabilities such as network attacks, manipulating and key management vulnerabilities, which may be used to compromise the entire election \cite{hajian2024blockchain}\cite{vladucu2023evoting}.

    \item \textbf{Client-Side Security and Malware:} Devices of the users may be breached by malware which manipulates or intercepts votes. A major issue is ensuring the integrity of the vote in case the endpoints are not secure \cite{jetir2025secureblockchain}\cite{ivoting-ccs2014}\cite{halderman2015ivote}.
    
    \item \textbf{Denial of Service Attacks:} The attacks can overload the network or the servers and slow down the voting process or make it impossible to vote \cite{jetir2025secureblockchain}\cite{prajapat2020investigating}.
\end{enumerate}

These challenges highlight the need for alternative approaches. Our research addresses this gap by combining zero-knowledge proofs (for mathematical privacy guarantees) with a purpose-built blockchain (for transparent, tamper-proof record keeping) to create a system where privacy and verifiability coexist without contradiction.

\section{Theoretical Foundations}

\subsection{Cryptographic Hash Functions}

A cryptographic hash function $H: \{0,1\}^* \rightarrow \{0,1\}^n$ is a function that takes an arbitrary length input and produces a fixed length output. In blockchain systems and zero-knowledge proof constructions, hash functions are basic primitives that are used as commitment functions, integrity checks, and as building blocks for more complex protocols \cite{merkle1988digital}. A secure hash function should have the following properties:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Preimage Resistance:} For a given $h$, it is hard to find $m$ such that $H(m) = h$. 
    
    \item \textbf{Second Preimage Resistance:} Given $m_1$, it is infeasible to find $m_2 \neq m_1$ such that $H(m_1) = H(m_2)$
    
    \item \textbf{Collision Resistance:} It is infeasible to find any pair $(m_1, m_2)$ where $m_1 \neq m_2$ and $H(m_1) = H(m_2)$.
\end{enumerate}

The selection of hash function is of great significance to zero-knowledge proof systems. Traditional hash functions optimized for hardware (such as SHA-256, Keccak) use bitwise operations, which are natural in binary circuits but very expensive as arithmetic constraints over prime fields, the native representation used by modern ZK proof systems.

\subsubsection{Poseidon Hash Function}

The Poseidon hash function, introduced by Grassi et al. (2021) \cite{grassi2021poseidon}, represents a paradigm shift in hash function design: rather than optimizing for hardware or software execution speed, Poseidon is designed specifically for efficient evaluation inside arithmetic circuits over prime fields. This design philosophy termed ``arithmetization-oriented''is a paradigm shift in the design of hash functions: it is not optimized for hardware or software evaluation speed; instead, it is optimized for efficient evaluation in arithmetic circuits over prime fields. This design philosophy, which we call “arithmetization-oriented” yields dramatic constraint count reductions compared to the traditional hash functions.

Poseidon is not a bit-level operation, it is a field-level operation:

\begin{equation}
\text{Poseidon}: \mathbb{F}_p^t \rightarrow \mathbb{F}_p
\label{eq:poseidon_def}
\end{equation}

Where $\mathbb{F}_p$ is a prime field (in our case, the BN254 scalar field with $p \approx 2^{254}$). The function is constructed using a sponge construction with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Round function:} Alternating $R_F$ full rounds and $R_P$ partial rounds. Full rounds apply the S-box to all state elements ; partial rounds apply it to one state element, reducing the totalnumber of constraints.
    \item \textbf{S-box:} $x \mapsto x^\alpha$ where $\alpha = 5$ for BN254. The selection of $\alpha$ such that $\gcd(\alpha, p-1) = 1$ (the S-box is a permutation over $\mathbb{F}_p$)  and the number of multiplications used is minimized: $x^5 = x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x$ requires only 3 multiplications ($x^2, x^4, x^5$).
    \item \textbf{Linear layer:} MDS (Maximum Distance Separable) matrix multiplication ensures optimal diffusion : each output element is dependent on all input elements in one round. The MDS property ensured that the minimum number of active S-boxes in any differential trail is maximized.
    \item \textbf{Round constants:} Pre-computed constants added to the beginning of each round to defeat algebraic attacks and to prevent symmetry.
\end{itemize}

The number of rounds will depend on the security analysis:
\begin{equation}
R_F = 8, \quad R_P = \begin{cases} 56 & \text{for } t = 2 \text{ (our commitment scheme)} \\ 57 & \text{for } t = 3 \text{ (Merkle tree hashing)} \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Security analysis:} Poseidon is analyzed against three types of attacks: (1) algebraic attacks based on the low-degree S-box, (2) differential and linear cryptanalysis based on the properties of the MDS matrix, and (3) interpolation attacks which are attempts to find low-degree polynomial representations. The numbers above are rounded to give a margin of security of at least 2× the minimum required for 128-bit security \cite{grassi2021poseidon}.

\subsection{Merkle Trees}

A Merkle tree \cite{merkle1988digital} is a basic data structure used in the design of cryptographic protocols, which offers efficient proofs of membership in a set of logarithmic complexity. It is a binary tree with each leaf node storing a hash of a data block, and each internal node storing a hash of the data block that is stored in its left child.

\begin{equation}
\text{node}_{i,j} = H(\text{node}_{i-1, 2j} \| \text{node}_{i-1, 2j+1})
\label{eq:merkle_node}
\end{equation}

i = level (0 = leaves) and j = position at level. The root (topmost node) is a small commitment for all data in the tree.

\textbf{Computational Complexity:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tree construction:} $O(n)$ hash computations for $n$ leaves
    \item \textbf{Membership proof generation:} $O(\log n)$-only $d$ sibling hashes needed, where $d = \lceil \log_2 n \rceil$
    \item \textbf{Proof verification:} $O(\log n)$-$d$ hash computations to reconstruct the root
    \item \textbf{Proof size:} $O(\log n)$-constant per level, logarithmic in total leaves
\end{itemize}

This logarithmic scaling is critical for ZK voting: regardless of whether the election has 100 or 100,000 registered voters, the ZK circuit performs only $d$ Poseidon hash computations to verify membership. With $d = 16$, our system supports up to $2^{16} = 65{,}536$ voters while maintaining constant proof size and verification time.

\subsubsection{Merkle Proof of Membership}

The prover provides evidence that a leaf $\ell$ exists at position $j$ in a tree with root $R$, by the \textit{authentication path} the sibling nodes along the path from $\ell$ to $R$:

\begin{equation}
\text{path} = [s_0, s_1, \ldots, s_{d-1}] \quad \text{where } d = \text{depth of tree}
\label{eq:merkle_path}
\end{equation}

The verifier reconstructs the root by iteratively hashing:

\begin{equation}
h_0 = \ell, \quad h_{i+1} = \begin{cases} H(h_i, s_i) & \text{if bit } i \text{ of } j = 0 \\ H(s_i, h_i) & \text{if bit } i \text{ of } j = 1 \end{cases}
\label{eq:merkle_verify}
\end{equation}

If $h_d = R$, then the leaf is proven to be in the tree. The proof size is $O(\log n)$ for a tree with $n$ leaves, only 16 hashes are needed for a tree with 65,536 leaves.

\subsubsection{Lean Incremental Merkle Tree}

Traditional Merkle trees require pre-allocation of all leaves. The \textit{Lean Incremental Merkle Tree} (LeanIMT) optimizes storage by only maintaining the ``frontier''; the rightmost node at each level(and computing internal nodes on-demand).It cuts down on-chain storage from $O(n)$ to $O(\log n)$.

\subsection{Non-Interactive Zero-Knowledge Proofs (NIZKs)}

Interactive ZK proofs \cite{goldwasser1985knowledge} involves two-way communication, blockchain applications need \textit{non-interactive} proofs ; a one way message from prover to verifier. The first approach to interactive to non-interactive was developed by Blum, Feldman, and Micali (1988) \cite{blum1988noninteractive} using a common reference string (CRS), and later by Fiat and Shamir \cite{fiat1986prove} using a random oracle (hash function) to simulate the verifier's challenges.

A NIZK proof system consists of three algorithms:

\begin{equation}
\pi \leftarrow \text{Prove}(pk, x, w) \quad ; \quad b \leftarrow \text{Verify}(vk, x, \pi)
\label{eq:nizk}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $pk$ - proving key (public parameters for proof generation)
    \item $vk$ - verification key (public parameters for proof checking)
    \item $x$ - public inputs (statement to be verified)
    \item $w$ - witness (private inputs known only to the prover)
    \item $\pi$ - proof (succinct, constant-size)
    \item $b$ - boolean verdict (accept/reject)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Succinctness} is a critical property: the size of the proof $|\pi|$ and the time needed to verify the proof should be much smaller than the time needed to compute the object being proven. Formally, for a relation $\mathcal{R}$ of size $n$, we need $|\pi| = O(\text{polylog}(n))$ and $T_{\text{verify}} = O(\text{polylog}(n))$. This property allows for practical deployment of blockchain: onchain verifier does constant time work for any circuit.

\subsubsection{Arithmetization: From Computation to Constraints}

The computation needs to be arithmetized to an arithmetic circuit over a finite field before a ZK proof can be created. There are various arithmetization schemes for modern proving systems:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R1CS (Rank-1 Constraint System):} Used by Groth16 and Circom. Represents constraints as $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{s} \times \vec{b} \cdot \vec{s} = \vec{c} \cdot \vec{s}$ where $\vec{s}$ is the witness vector. One multiplication gate is represented by each constraint.
    
    \item \textbf{PLONKish Arithmetization:} Used by PLONK \cite{gabizon2019plonk} and UltraHonk \cite{aztec2024ultrahonk}. Represents constraints as entries in a \textit{trace table} with custom gates. Each row encodes a gate: $q_L \cdot w_L + q_R \cdot w_R + q_O \cdot w_O + q_M \cdot w_L \cdot w_R + q_C = 0$, where $q_*$ are selector polynomials and $w_*$ are wire values. This is more flexible than R1CS because:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Custom gates can encode complex operations (e.g., range checks, lookups) in fewer rows
        \item Lookup tables enable efficient implementation of non-arithmetic operations
        \item The universal reference string (URS) is circuit-independent
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Our system uses UltraHonk's PLONKish arithmetization via the Noir compiler \cite{noir2024docs}, which automatically translates high-level Noir code into an optimized constraint system. The compiled circuit contains 19,278 gates,which is significantly less than the number of gates in an equivalent Circom circuit, thanks to the custom gates that UltraHonk has created for Poseidon and range checks.

\subsubsection{ZK-SNARKs vs.\ ZK-STARKs}

The two most prominent families of succinct zero-knowledge proof systems have very different cryptographic assumptions and properties:

\textbf{ZK-SNARKs} (Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-interactive Arguments of Knowledge) \cite{groth2016size} are based on bilinear pairings over elliptic curves (e.g., BN254, BLS12-381). They producethe smallest proofs (128 bytes for Groth16) but have traditionally needed a \textit{trusted setup}, trusted setup, which is a ceremony that produces structured reference strings (SRS). In the event of a compromise, an adversary can create proofs that can be verified, and thus defeat the system entirely. Recent pairing-based systems (PLONK, UltraHonk) remove the trusted setups per circuit by employing a universal or transparent reference string \cite{gabizon2019plonk}.

\textbf{ZK-STARKs} (Zero-Knowledge Scalable Transparent Arguments of Knowledge) are based on hash functions only, therefore they are transparent (no trusted setup) and may be post-quantum secure. They have much larger proofs (100 KB vs. 128 bytes for Groth16), but are faster to verify.

\textbf{UltraHonk} (our choice) represents a middle ground: it is a pairing-based system that does not require a trusted setup, uses a transparent commitment scheme (IPA or KZG with powers-of-tau) and has moderately-sized proofs (14 KB) and fast verification that is suitable for on-chain deployment. The important thing to note is that no party has to be trusted during system initialization, which is a critical attribute for election integrity, given the transparent setup of UltraHonk. \cite{aztec2024ultrahonk}.

The Semaphore protocol \cite{semaphore2020} provides a closely related ZK-based anonymous signaling primitive on Ethereum. Our adoption of UltraHonk avoids the trusted setup challenge entirely while maintaining efficient on-chain verification.

\subsection{Privacy Models in E-Voting}

It is important to be familiar with various privacy models, to be able to assess e-voting systems. There are three degrees of privacy guarantees:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Computational Privacy (Encryption-based):}  Privacy is guaranteed as long as the adversary cannot defeat the underlying encryption scheme. Violation of privacy is possible by a powerful adversary such as having access to key materials for decryption or access to a quantum computer. Estonia (RSA encryption), homomorphic tallying systems.
    
    \item \textbf{Information-Theoretic Privacy (Secret Sharing):} Privacy is achieved even if there is no computation power, with the proviso that a number of servers (more than a threshold) remain honest. Example: schemes that require k of-n servers to collude in threshold decryption.
    
    \item \textbf{Zero-Knowledge Privacy (Our Approach):}  The proof contains no information other than the truth of the statement. There is no decryption key. Even if the vote taker is a computational unbounded adversary, the adversary learns nothing about the identity of the voter from the proof. The privacy guarantee is based on the zero knowledge property of the proof system (information theoretical in the random oracle model (ROM)).
\end{enumerate}

This is important because in encryption-based systems (models 1 and 2) some party or some threshold of parties can, in principle, decrypt votes. In our ZK based system there is none, the voter's identity is never encrypted, it is never revealed.

Electronic voting systems on blockchain have grown to be studied extensively due to the limitations of traditional voting systems particularly to the areas of security, transparency, verifiability, and decentralization. \subsubsection{ZK-SNARK-Based Ticket Voting System}

Rabia et al. suggested an electronic voting system based on blockchain technology that uses Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-Interactive Arguments of Knowledge (zk-SNARKs) and a ticket-based authentication scheme \cite{rabia2024zksnark}. They have a combination of blockchain technology, smart contracts, Merkle trees, and zero-knowledge proofs to improve voter privacy, anonymity, and the integrity of the electoral process.

Here, the hashed voter tickets are stored in a Merkle tree; and the zk-SNARK proofs enable voters to demonstrate that they are eligible to vote without revealing their identity or vote selections. The architecture features a front-end user interface for voter interaction, a back-end service that generates the zk-SNARK proofs, smart contracts for tallying and verifying the votes, and a Merkle tree to verify voter tickets.

The voting process contains four phases: setup, registration, voting and counting. In the registration period, eligible voters are allocated unique voting tickets and their unique voting tickets are hashed and added to the Merkle tree. In the voting phase, voters create zk-SNARK proofs showing that they have a valid unused voting ticket and they then cast their encrypted vote to the blockchain for verification.

The system introduces several important features:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Merkle tree integration enables efficient ticket verification with reduced on-chain storage requirements.
    
    \item Zero-knowledge authentication provides verifiability, while retaining the anonymity of voters.
    
    \item Deterministic ticket verification can be used to eliminate the double voting issue.
    
    \item Blockchain-based storage provides immutable and transparent election records.
\end{enumerate}

However, the authors also acknowledge several limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System is primarily a conceptual proof-of-concept and not a full-scale implementation.
    
    \item zk-SNARK proof generation introduces computational computational complexity and scalability issues, particularly for large-scale elections.
    
    \item The system does not provide detailed blockchain consensus or distributed synchronization mechanisms.
    
    \item The cryptographic workflow could cause user experience problems for non-technical users.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{ZK Voting: Nullifiable Commitment-Based Coercion-Resistant System}

Park et al. proposed \textbf{zkVoting},  a zero-knowledge blockchain-based e-voting system with a nullifiable commitment scheme which is resistant to coercion \cite{park2024zkvoting}. Unlike previous fake-credential voting schemes (like JCJ and Civitas), zkVoting maintains end-to-end verifiability and voter anonymity with efficient tallying, O(n), complexity.

The core innovation of the system is the \textbf{nullifiable commitment scheme}.One way is for each voter to get one real commitment key and multiple fake commitment keys from the authority. When tallying, the commitments made with the real key are not lost, but those made with fake keys are lost and are treated as zero. This technique enables voters to ward off attempts at coercion by handing out fake keys to attackers and then later using the real key to vote.

The zkVoting architecture consists of four phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Setup Phase:} The authority generates public parameters, master keys, encryption keys, and zero-knowledge proof parameters.
    
    \item \textbf{Registration Phase:} Voters authenticate themselves and obtain either real or fake casting keys.  The authority also issues proofs to make sure that no two eligible voters receive a copy of the same key.
    
    \item \textbf{Voting Phase:} every voter encrypts the ballot, casts a commitment with their casting key, and provides the voter, the ballot, and the correct construction of the commitment as proofs of ballot validity, voter eligibility, and the correct construction of the commitment.
    
    \item \textbf{Tally Phase:} The authority decrypts ballots and applies the nullification process. Fake commitments are removed automatically, and only valid ballots are counted.
\end{enumerate}

The system provides several important security guarantees:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Ballots are encrypted using the hybrid approach with zero-knowledge proofs to ensure ballot privacy
    \item Voter anonymity ensured by cryptographic commitments and serial numbers
    \item Receipt freeness and coercion resistance through fake casting keys
    \item End-to-end verifiability by using publicly verifiable proofs
\end{itemize}

zkVoting was implemented using Groth16 zk-SNARKs, MiMC7 hashing, Merkle tree membership proofs, and Ethereum smart contracts. Results of experiments demonstrated the practical performance: the time to generate the ballots ranged from 1.6 to 7.3 seconds, depending on the device used. The number of gas used for ballot verification on Ethereum was about 347,000  \cite{park2024zkvoting}.

Although zkVoting significantly improves coercion resistance and tally efficiency compared to previous systems, it also has a number of drawbacks:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The system relies on Groth16 trusted setup ceremonies, which could be a security risk if they are compromised. 
    
    \item Deployment on Ethereum introduces high gas costs for ballot verification.
    
    \item The methodology also relies on the trust of the registrar and the tally authority for some privacy assurances.
    
    \item The implementation depends on dedicated applications rather than lightweight browser-based applications.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Gaps in Literature}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Client-Side Proof Generation:} Most proposed ZK-voting systems focus primarily on conceptual architectures and server-assisted proof workflows rather than fully decentralized browser-based proof generation accessible to ordinary users, but few demonstrate complete browser-based proof generation accessible to ordinary users \cite{mdpi2024evoting, vladucu2023evoting}.

    \item \textbf{Purpose-Built Blockchain:} Most systems deploy on Ethereum or Hyperledger, inheriting their complexity (gas fees, wallet management, EVM overhead). No existing work combines a custom election-specific blockchain with ZK proofs.
    
    \item \textbf{Trusted Setup Elimination:} Systems using Groth16 (Circom/snarkjs) require trusted setup ceremonies that are difficult to verify and represent an attack vector.
    
    \item \textbf{Usability:} Insufficient attention is given to user experience, voters should not need to understand cryptography or manage cryptocurrency wallets \cite{mdpi2024evoting, vladucu2023evoting}.

    \item \textbf{Trusted Setup and Blockchain Cost Barriers:} Recent advanced coercion-resistant systems such as zkVoting achieve strong privacy and efficient tallying through novel cryptographic primitives. However, these systems still depend on Groth16 trusted setup ceremonies and public blockchains with high gas costs, creating both security and economic limitations for large-scale deployment \cite{park2024zkvoting}.
\end{enumerate}

Our implementation addresses all five gaps by providing: a functional prototype with browser-based proof generation (Gap 1\&2), a custom Go blockchain (Gap 3), UltraHonk proving with no trusted setup (Gap 4), and an intuitive web interface requiring no wallet extensions (Gap 5).

\subsection{Commitment Scheme}

A \textit{commitment scheme} allows a party to commit to a value while keeping it hidden, with the ability to reveal it later. Our scheme uses Poseidon hashing:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{C = \text{Poseidon}_2(\text{nullifier}, \text{secret})}
\label{eq:commitment}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\text{nullifier} \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{F}_p$ --- a cryptographically random field element (256 bits of entropy)
    \item $\text{secret} \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{F}_p$ --- an independent random field element
    \item $C \in \mathbb{F}_p$ --- the commitment (publicly stored on-chain)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Security Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Binding:} Given $C$, it is computationally infeasible to find $(n', s') \neq (n, s)$ such that $\text{Poseidon}_2(n', s') = C$ (collision resistance of Poseidon)
    \item \textbf{Hiding:} Given $C$, it reveals no information about $n$ or $s$ (preimage resistance of Poseidon)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Numerical Example:}

Let us trace through with concrete (simplified) values to illustrate the scheme:
\begin{align}
\text{nullifier} &= 0x1a2b3c\ldots \quad \text{(random 254-bit field element)} \\
\text{secret} &= 0x4d5e6f\ldots \quad \text{(independent random element)} \\
C &= \text{Poseidon}_2(0x1a2b3c\ldots, 0x4d5e6f\ldots) = 0x7f8a9b\ldots
\end{align}

The commitment $C$ is stored on-chain. The voter retains $(n, s)$ privately in their browser's localStorage.

\subsection{Nullifier Scheme}

To prevent double-voting without revealing which commitment is voting:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{N_h = \text{Poseidon}_1(\text{nullifier})}
\label{eq:nullifier}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why this works:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each voter has exactly one nullifier (generated during registration)
    \item Each nullifier produces exactly one nullifier hash $N_h$ (deterministic)
    \item When voting, $N_h$ is revealed publicly and stored on-chain
    \item If the same voter tries to vote again, the contract detects the duplicate $N_h$
    \item Given only $N_h$, finding the original nullifier requires breaking Poseidon's preimage resistance
    \item The commitment $C = \text{Poseidon}_2(n, s)$ and the nullifier hash $N_h = \text{Poseidon}_1(n)$ are \textit{unlinkable} without knowledge of $n$
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{images/m2_nullifier_unlinkability.png}
\caption{Nullifier scheme: the voter's private nullifier $n$ produces two public values---$N_h$ (for double-vote prevention) and $C$ (for membership registration). Without knowing $n$, an observer cannot link them.}
\label{fig:nullifier_scheme}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Merkle Tree Membership Proof}

All voter commitments are stored in a Lean Incremental Merkle Tree using Poseidon as the internal hash:

\begin{equation}
\text{parent} = \text{Poseidon}_2(\text{left\_child}, \text{right\_child})
\label{eq:merkle_parent}
\end{equation}

The tree supports up to $2^{16} = 65{,}536$ leaves (voters). To prove membership, the voter provides:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Their commitment (recomputed from nullifier + secret inside the circuit)
    \item Their leaf index $j$ (private---keeps the voter's position hidden)
    \item The sibling hashes $[s_0, s_1, \ldots, s_{15}]$ along the path from leaf to root (private)
\end{itemize}

The circuit recomputes the root using Equation~\ref{eq:merkle_verify} and asserts it matches the public on-chain root.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{images/m2_merkle_tree_membership.png}
    \caption{Merkle tree membership proof: the voter proves their commitment exists as a leaf by providing the sibling hashes along the path from their leaf to the root, without revealing which leaf is theirs}
    \label{fig:merkle_membership}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Combined ZK Statement}

The complete statement proven by our ZK circuit can be expressed as:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\text{Given public inputs } (N_h, R, v, d): \\
&\exists \, (n, s, j, \vec{S}) \text{ such that:} \\
&\quad (1) \; N_h = \text{Poseidon}_1(n) \\
&\quad (2) \; C = \text{Poseidon}_2(n, s) \\
&\quad (3) \; \text{MerkleVerify}(C, j, \vec{S}, d) = R \\
&\quad (4) \; v \in \{0, 1\}
\end{aligned}
}
\label{eq:zk_statement}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $N_h$ --- nullifier hash (public, prevents double-voting)
    \item $R$ --- Merkle root (public, ensures proof is for the current tree state)
    \item $v$ --- vote choice (public, bound to the proof so it cannot be changed)
    \item $d$ --- tree depth (public)
    \item $n$ --- nullifier (private)
    \item $s$ --- secret (private)
    \item $j$ --- leaf index (private, protects voter position)
    \item $\vec{S}$ --- sibling array (private, Merkle authentication path)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Formal Threat Model}

We define the adversary model and security assumptions under which our system provides its guarantees.

\subsubsection{Adversary Capabilities}

We consider a computationally bounded adversary $\mathcal{A}$ (probabilistic polynomial-time) with the following capabilities:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Network observation:} $\mathcal{A}$ can observe all on-chain transactions, including proof data, nullifier hashes, vote values, and transaction metadata (sender address, timestamp, gas price).
    
    \item \textbf{Partial system control:} $\mathcal{A}$ may control some voter accounts (Sybil attack) but cannot control the contract owner account or the blockchain consensus mechanism.
    
    \item \textbf{Proof manipulation:} $\mathcal{A}$ may attempt to forge proofs, replay valid proofs with altered public inputs, or extract private witness data from observed proofs.
    
    \item \textbf{Smart contract interaction:} $\mathcal{A}$ can call any public function on the deployed contracts and analyze the contract bytecode.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Security Assumptions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Poseidon hash security:} The Poseidon hash function over BN254 provides at least 128 bits of security against preimage, second preimage, and collision attacks \cite{grassi2021poseidon}.
    
    \item \textbf{Discrete logarithm hardness:} The elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem on BN254 is computationally intractable for PPT adversaries. This underpins the soundness of the UltraHonk proof system.
    
    \item \textbf{Random oracle model:} The Fiat-Shamir heuristic (used to make proofs non-interactive) is modeled as a random oracle. The zero-knowledge property holds information-theoretically in this model.
    
    \item \textbf{Client-side integrity:} The voter's browser and local environment are not compromised by malware during proof generation. This is a standard assumption shared by all client-side cryptographic systems.
    
    \item \textbf{Blockchain liveness:} The underlying blockchain (Hardhat local / custom Go chain) processes transactions in a timely manner and does not censor valid transactions.
\end{enumerate}

Given the above adversary and assumptions, our system provides:

\subsubsection{Attacks Outside the Threat Model}

The following attacks are acknowledged but outside our current security scope:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quantum adversary:} BN254 is not post-quantum secure. A quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could break the discrete logarithm assumption. Migration to lattice-based or hash-based proof systems would be required.
    
    \item \textbf{Client-side malware:} If the voter's browser is compromised, the malware could alter the vote before proof generation or exfiltrate private keys. This is a fundamental limitation of any client-side system \cite{ivoting-ccs2014, halderman2015ivote}.
    
    \item \textbf{Coercion with physical presence:} If a coercer physically observes the voter's screen during voting, the ZK proof cannot prevent observation of the vote choice.
    
    \item \textbf{Side-channel attacks:} Timing or power analysis of the proof generation process is not considered (mitigated by constant-time WASM execution in practice).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Why Each Input's Visibility Matters}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{nullifier\_hash is public:} The contract stores it to detect repeat votes. Making it public does not compromise privacy because deriving the original nullifier from its Poseidon hash requires $O(2^{254})$ operations.
    
    \item \textbf{root is public:} The contract compares it against the current on-chain root to ensure the proof corresponds to the latest voter registry state.
    
    \item \textbf{vote is public:} The contract reads this to increment the correct counter. Binding it as a public input inside the proof prevents an attacker from reusing a proof with a different vote value.
    
    \item \textbf{index is private:} If the index were public, an observer could determine which leaf (which registration) is voting---breaking anonymity entirely.
    
    \item \textbf{siblings are private:} The Merkle path uniquely identifies the voter's position in the tree.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why rebuild the Merkle tree in the browser?}

The smart contract stores the Merkle tree in a frontier format (only the rightmost node at each level). There is no efficient on-chain function to retrieve arbitrary Merkle proofs. Therefore, the frontend:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Fetches all \texttt{NewLeaf} events (containing leaf values and indices)
    \item Reconstructs the complete tree using the same Poseidon hash function
    \item Generates the inclusion proof locally
\end{enumerate}

The \texttt{@zk-kit/lean-imt} TypeScript library produces identical tree structures to its Solidity counterpart, ensuring the locally-computed root always matches the on-chain root.


\subsection{Anonymous Vote Submission}

\subsubsection{The Linkability Problem}

If the voter submits the proof from the same blockchain address they registered with, an on-chain observer could trivially link the registration transaction to the vote transaction---breaking anonymity despite the ZK proof.

\subsubsection{Solution: Identity Separation via Burner Wallets}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generate fresh wallet:} \texttt{generatePrivateKey()} creates a cryptographically random Ethereum private key (no prior transaction history)
    \item \textbf{Fund the wallet:} On local Hardhat, \texttt{setBalance} provides gas. On production, a relay/paymaster would be used.
    \item \textbf{Submit vote:} The fresh wallet calls \texttt{Voting.vote(proof, publicInputs)}
    \item \textbf{Discard:} The wallet is no longer needed after voting
\end{enumerate}

On the custom blockchain (Track B), this problem is eliminated entirely---there are no wallets or gas fees; the ZK proof itself serves as authentication.

\section{Analysis of Privacy Guarantees}

Our system provides \textit{zero-knowledge privacy} rather than \textit{computational privacy}---a fundamental distinction with significant implications for election integrity:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Encryption-based systems} (e.g., Estonia \cite{madise2005evoting}): A key holder can eventually decrypt---privacy depends on the honesty of the key custodian. Formally, privacy reduces to the semantic security of the encryption scheme: $\text{Privacy} \iff \text{IND-CPA security of } \mathcal{E}$.
    \item \textbf{Mix-net systems} (e.g., Switzerland \cite{federalchancellery_evoting}): Privacy depends on at least one mix server being honest (threshold assumption). If all $k$ mix servers collude, all votes are deanonymized.
    \item \textbf{Homomorphic tallying} (e.g., Helios \cite{adida2008helios}): Votes are encrypted homomorphically and tallied without decryption of individual votes. Privacy depends on the threshold decryption committee not colluding.
    \item \textbf{Our ZK-based system}: No entity, regardless of computational power, can link a vote to a voter. The ZK proof reveals \textit{mathematically nothing} beyond the truth of the statement. Formally, for any PPT distinguisher $\mathcal{D}$:
\end{itemize}

\begin{equation}
|\Pr[\mathcal{D}(\pi, x) = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{D}(S(x), x) = 1]| \leq \text{negl}(\lambda)
\end{equation}

where $\pi$ is the real proof and $S(x)$ is the simulated proof (produced without knowledge of the witness). This means the proof contains \textit{zero} information about the voter's identity.

\section{Formal Correctness Analysis}

We analyze the correctness of our protocol against the formal voting requirements established in Chapter 1.

\subsection{Eligibility Correctness}

\textbf{Claim:} Only voters whose commitments are in the Merkle tree can generate valid proofs.

\textbf{Argument:} Suppose an adversary $\mathcal{A}$ can generate a proof $\pi$ that passes verification for a commitment $C^*$ not in the tree. Then $\mathcal{A}$ has found values $(n^*, s^*, j^*, \vec{S}^*)$ such that $\text{MerkleVerify}(C^*, j^*, \vec{S}^*, d) = R$ where $R$ is the current root. But the Merkle tree is collision-resistant (under Poseidon's collision resistance): the probability that a non-member commitment produces a valid path to the root is bounded by the collision probability of Poseidon, which is $\leq 2^{-128}$.

\subsection{Uniqueness Correctness}

\textbf{Claim:} Each voter can vote at most once.

\textbf{Argument:} The nullifier $n$ is fixed at registration time. The nullifier hash $N_h = \text{Poseidon}_1(n)$ is deterministic. On the first vote, $N_h$ is stored on-chain. On any subsequent attempt, the contract checks $\texttt{s\_nullifierHashes}[N_h]$ and reverts. To vote twice, $\mathcal{A}$ would need to find $n' \neq n$ such that $\text{Poseidon}_1(n') = N_h$ (second preimage) or find a commitment in the tree linked to a different nullifier (which requires registration of a second commitment, prevented by the \texttt{s\_hasRegistered} check).

\subsection{Vote Binding Correctness}

\textbf{Claim:} The vote value cannot be altered after proof generation.

\textbf{Argument:} The vote $v$ is a public input to the ZK circuit. The proof $\pi$ commits to the specific value of $v$ through the Fiat-Shamir transcript. If an adversary modifies $v$ to $v'$ after proof generation, the verifier recomputes the challenge using the altered public inputs, and the proof verification fails. This follows directly from the soundness property of UltraHonk: $\Pr[\text{Verify}(vk, x', \pi) = 1 \wedge x' \neq x] \leq \text{negl}(\lambda)$.

\section{Limitations, Challenges, and Future Work}

\subsection{Current Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gas Costs (resolved by Track B):} On-chain ZK verification costs 300K--500K gas per vote. At Ethereum mainnet gas prices (as of 2024, approximately 20--50 gwei), each vote would cost \$2--\$10 USD---prohibitive for public elections. The custom blockchain eliminates gas entirely.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Candidate Support:} Current system supports Yes/No. Extension to multiple candidates requires circuit modification (vote as integer with range check: $v \in \{0, 1, \ldots, k-1\}$ for $k$ candidates). This adds approximately $\lceil \log_2 k \rceil$ additional constraints to the circuit.
    
    \item \textbf{Voter Authentication:} The allowlist-based system requires manual voter addition. Integration with decentralized identity (Worldcoin, government eID) is future work. This is a deployment challenge rather than a cryptographic one.
    
    \item \textbf{Coercion Resistance:} While receipt-free (the voter cannot prove to a third party how they voted), the system does not implement re-voting for stronger coercion resistance. Adding re-voting would require modifying the nullifier scheme to allow multiple nullifier hashes per voter while only counting the last vote.
    
    \item \textbf{Root Freshness:} If new voters register between proof generation and vote submission, the root changes and the proof becomes invalid. A root history mechanism (storing the last $k$ valid roots) would solve this at the cost of $O(k)$ additional storage.
    
    \item \textbf{Custom Blockchain Completion:} Stages 2--7 of Track B are in active development by team members.
    
    \item \textbf{Post-Quantum Security:} The BN254 elliptic curve used by UltraHonk is vulnerable to Shor's algorithm. Migration to lattice-based or hash-based proof systems (e.g., STARKs) would be necessary for long-term quantum resistance, at the cost of larger proofs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Technical Challenges Encountered}

During the implementation, several significant technical challenges were identified and resolved:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hash Function Compatibility:} Poseidon hash implementations must produce identical outputs across three environments: Noir circuits (field arithmetic), Solidity smart contracts (uint256), and TypeScript frontend (BigInt). Any discrepancy in round constants, field modulus, or endianness causes proof verification failure. We resolved this by using the \texttt{@zk-kit} library family, which provides verified cross-platform Poseidon implementations.
    
    \item \textbf{WASM Memory Constraints:} The Barretenberg WASM module requires approximately 256 MB of memory for proof generation. Some mobile browsers and older devices cannot allocate this, limiting accessibility. We mitigate this by detecting WASM support and memory availability before attempting proof generation.
    
    \item \textbf{Circuit Compilation Toolchain:} The Noir/Barretenberg toolchain requires WSL (Windows Subsystem for Linux) on Windows systems, adding development complexity. The \texttt{--oracle\_hash keccak} flag for Ethereum-compatible verifier generation was not documented in the main Noir documentation, requiring examination of the Barretenberg source code.
    
    \item \textbf{Merkle Tree Frontier Format:} The on-chain LeanIMT stores only frontier nodes (the rightmost path), not the complete tree. This design minimizes gas costs but means the frontend must reconstruct the tree from event logs---a non-obvious architectural requirement that was discovered during integration testing.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Research Directions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formal Verification:} Applying formal methods (e.g., model checking, theorem proving) to verify the correctness of the ZK circuit and smart contract logic. Tools such as Certora or K Framework could provide machine-checked proofs of contract invariants.
    
    \item \textbf{Differential Privacy for Tallies:} While individual votes are private, the final tally reveals aggregate information. Adding differential privacy noise to intermediate tallies could provide privacy for the aggregate result, though this trades accuracy for privacy.
    
    \item \textbf{Recursive Proofs for Scalability:} Using recursive proof composition (proof-of-proofs) to batch-verify multiple votes in a single on-chain transaction, reducing gas costs by amortizing verification across $k$ votes.
    
    \item \textbf{Decentralized Identity Integration:} Replacing the allowlist mechanism with privacy-preserving identity verification (e.g., ZK credentials from government-issued documents) to enable truly self-sovereign voter registration.
    
    \item \textbf{Cross-Chain Deployment:} Evaluating deployment on Layer 2 networks (Arbitrum, Optimism, zkSync) where gas costs are 10--100x lower than Ethereum mainnet, as an alternative to the custom blockchain for organizations that prefer existing infrastructure.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Conclusion}

This project presents the design, implementation, and analysis of a privacy-preserving end-to-end verifiable electronic voting system that addresses the fundamental tension between ballot secrecy and election integrity. Through the novel combination of zero-knowledge proofs, on-chain verification, and cryptographic identity separation, the system achieves simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Complete voter anonymity} --- through zero-knowledge proofs and identity separation, no entity (including system administrators) can determine which voter cast which vote. This is a \textit{mathematical} guarantee, not a policy guarantee. The privacy is information-theoretic in the random oracle model, meaning no computational advance can break it.
    
    \item \textbf{Double-vote prevention} --- through deterministic nullifier hashing, each voter can vote exactly once. Attempting a second vote reveals the same nullifier hash, which is rejected. The determinism is guaranteed by the preimage resistance of the Poseidon hash function.
    
    \item \textbf{Public verifiability} --- all proof verifications and vote counts are transparent on-chain. Anyone can independently verify the election result by replaying the transaction history and confirming that every accepted proof passes the HonkVerifier contract.
    
    \item \textbf{Client-side privacy} --- proof generation happens entirely in the voter's browser via WebAssembly. No server, no administrator, no third party ever sees the voter's private inputs. This eliminates the need for trusted intermediaries that characterize encryption-based systems \cite{madise2005evoting, federalchancellery_evoting}.
    
    \item \textbf{No trusted setup} --- the UltraHonk proving system \cite{aztec2024ultrahonk} eliminates trusted setup ceremonies, removing a significant attack vector present in Groth16-based systems \cite{groth2016size}. The system parameters are derived from publicly verifiable randomness.
    
    \item \textbf{No trusted authority for privacy} --- unlike encryption-based systems (where a key holder can decrypt) or mix-net systems (where colluding mixers can deanonymize), our ZK-based approach provides privacy without any trust assumption on third parties.
\end{enumerate}

The system was built across 10 iterative phases (Track A) with all phases completed and demonstrated successfully. Additionally, a purpose-built blockchain (Track B) is under parallel development to eliminate the overhead of general-purpose Ethereum infrastructure---removing gas fees, wallet requirements, and EVM complexity while preserving all cryptographic guarantees.

\textbf{Research Contributions.} This project makes the following contributions to the field of cryptographic voting systems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A complete, functional implementation of browser-based ZK proof generation for voting, demonstrating that client-side proof generation is practical with modern hardware and WASM technology.
    \item A formal analysis of the security properties achieved by combining Poseidon-based commitment schemes with UltraHonk proofs and on-chain verification.
    \item A comparative evaluation against existing e-voting systems (Estonia, iVote, Swiss Post, FASTEN), identifying the specific privacy and trust advantages of the ZK-based approach.
    \item A dual-track architecture that separates cryptographic correctness (Track A) from deployment infrastructure (Track B), enabling independent development and testing of each layer.
    \item Empirical measurements of proof generation time, proof size, gas costs, and scalability characteristics that inform the practical deployability of ZK-based voting.
\end{enumerate}

The project demonstrates that modern zero-knowledge proof systems---particularly the Noir language \cite{noir2024docs} and UltraHonk proving backend---have matured to the point where practical, usable, privacy-preserving voting is achievable with open-source tools and standard web browsers. The formal requirements of secure electronic voting (eligibility, uniqueness, privacy, verifiability) can be simultaneously satisfied through careful cryptographic protocol design without relying on trusted third parties.

Future work includes completing the custom blockchain integration (Stages 2--7), adding multi-candidate support via range-checked circuit inputs, conducting formal verification of the smart contract invariants, and evaluating post-quantum alternatives to the BN254 curve used by UltraHonk.
